# Berlin Rental Property Investment Notes

_Last updated: 14 April 2026_

## 1. The short version

My honest conclusion is this:

**A rental property in Berlin can make sense, but for most private buyers in 2026 it is not automatically a good investment.**

The main reason is simple:

- Berlin is expensive to buy into.
- Rental regulation is real.
- Existing rents are often much lower than portal asking rents.
- Tax benefits help, but they do **not** rescue a weak deal.

So the right question is not:

> How much tax can I get back?

The right question is:

> Does the deal still make sense after realistic rent, financing, taxes, and boring ownership costs?

That is the core idea behind everything we discussed.

---

## 2. Why Berlin often feels too expensive

Berlin still has strong housing demand and a structural housing shortage, which supports rents over the long term. But that does **not** mean every apartment is a good investment.

### 2.1 Buy-in costs are high

When you buy in Berlin, the purchase price is only the beginning.

Typical acquisition costs include:

- **Grunderwerbsteuer:** 6% in Berlin
- **Notary + land register:** often around 2%
- **Broker fee:** where applicable, buyer-side costs commonly fall in the low-single-digit range and can still be material

That means a buyer can easily spend **8% to 11%+ on top of the purchase price**, before even thinking about renovation, financing setup, or reserves.

This is why a property can look acceptable on paper and still feel heavy in real life.

### 2.2 Yields are often thin

Recent Berlin market data shows that resale apartment prices recovered in 2025 to around **€5,130/m²** on average, while asking rents for existing apartments were reported around **€16.35/m²**. On headline numbers like that, gross yields are only in the mid-3% range before real-life costs.[7]

That is thin.

It gets worse once you include:

- acquisition costs
- non-recoverable housegeld
- vacancy risk
- maintenance
- admin costs
- financing friction

### 2.3 Existing rents and new asking rents are very different

This is one of the biggest traps.

Berlin market reports show that **existing rents are far below new asking rents**. One 2025 report notes that existing rents in privately or institutionally rented apartments are on average **below €9/m²**, while new-contract rents average **€15.79/m²**.[8]

That means many already-rented apartments look better in listings than they really are.

If a property is already rented, the **actual current cold rent** matters much more than some optimistic “market rent” story.

### 2.4 Regulation matters

Berlin extended the **Mietpreisbremse** through the end of **2029**.[6]

That does not mean rents can never rise, but it does mean the upside is more limited than many first-time investors assume.

If your deal only works because you assume strong rent increases soon after purchase, that is a warning sign.

## 3. Why the tax angle is attractive for a high-income earner

Your thinking is understandable.

If you have a strong salary and already pay a lot of income tax, a rental property can create deductible expenses that reduce your taxable income.

That part is real.

### 3.1 Rental income has its own tax bucket

Income from renting out property falls under **Einkünfte aus Vermietung und Verpachtung** under §21 EStG.[1]

Expenses connected to earning that rental income are generally treated as **Werbungskosten** under §9 EStG.[2]

So if the property produces a tax loss, that loss can reduce your overall taxable income, subject to the general tax rules.

### 3.2 Why this is useful for someone with a higher income

If you are in a relatively high marginal tax bracket, each deductible euro is worth more to you than it is to someone in a much lower bracket.

That means a rental property can be more tax-efficient for you than for someone with lower taxable income.

This is why many well-paid employees look at rental property as a tax shield.

And to be clear:

**that logic is not stupid.**

It is valid.

But it becomes dangerous when tax savings become the main reason to buy.

---

## 4. The biggest mistake: mixing up tax loss and cash flow

This is the single most important concept.

A property can look good on a tax return and still be weak in your bank account.

### 4.1 Interest is deductible, principal is not

Mortgage **interest** connected to the rental property is generally deductible as a cost of earning rental income.[2]

But **repayment of principal** is not a normal current deduction.

This matters a lot.

Because in real life you pay both:

- interest
- repayment

But for tax, only the interest helps you immediately.

So a property can feel “tax efficient” while still consuming a lot of monthly cash.

### 4.2 A tax refund does not mean the state paid the loss

Example:

- Suppose your property creates a **€10,000 tax loss**.
- Suppose your effective marginal tax effect is roughly **42%**.
- Your tax saving might be around **€4,200**.

That sounds nice.

But economically, you still lost about **€5,800** yourself.

So the state helped with part of the pain. It did not eliminate the pain.

That is why the correct mindset is:

**Tax benefits improve a decent deal. They do not turn a bad deal into a good one.**

## 5. What you can typically deduct, and where people get confused

The exact tax treatment depends on the facts and on your tax advisor’s structuring, but the broad logic is:

### Usually relevant deductible items

- mortgage interest linked to the rental property
- ongoing management / administration costs
- insurance linked to the property
- property-related taxes and public charges connected to rental income
- certain repair and maintenance costs
- tax advisor fees related to rental income matters
- depreciation on the **building** portion

### Common points of confusion

#### 5.1 The whole purchase price is not depreciable

Depreciation is based on the **building** portion, not the land portion. In practice, the total purchase cost usually has to be split between land and building before AfA is calculated.

#### 5.2 Upfront acquisition costs are not “free money back”

Transfer tax, notary, land register, and similar buying costs are real cash outflows. They affect your economics even if part of the cost becomes relevant for tax over time.

#### 5.3 Housegeld is not one simple tax number

People often treat housegeld like a single clean deduction. That is not how it works.

Some components may be economically relevant and tax-relevant, but reserve contributions are a special issue.

---

## 6. Depreciation (AfA): useful, but often oversold

Depreciation is one of the main reasons rental property can be tax-efficient.

Under the current rules in §7 EStG, standard straight-line building depreciation generally follows this pattern:[3]

- **3%** for residential buildings completed **after 31 December 2022**
- **2%** for buildings completed from **1925 through 2022**
- **2.5%** for buildings completed **before 1925**

This can be meaningful.

But there are two important realities:

1. AfA applies to the **building basis**, not to the entire purchase price.
2. AfA is a tax tool, not a cash inflow.

So yes, AfA can improve your after-tax result.

But if the property is overpriced, AfA alone does not save it.

---

## 7. The WEG reserve trap: Erhaltungsrücklage

This is a very important point for apartments in Germany.

A lot of buyers assume that when they pay into the WEG maintenance reserve, they can deduct it immediately.

That is **not** how it currently works.

The BFH decided in 2025 that payments into the **Erhaltungsrücklage** of a WEG are **not immediately deductible Werbungskosten** simply because the owner paid them. The deduction generally comes only when the reserve money is actually spent for concrete maintenance measures.[5]

Why this matters:

- housegeld can feel high
- some of it may be reserve funding
- that part may not help your tax return right away

So when you analyze an apartment, you should always separate:

- current running costs
- reserve contributions
- truly non-recoverable ownership costs

---

## 8. The renovation trap: the 15% rule in the first 3 years

Another major trap is post-purchase renovation.

Under §6 EStG, repair and modernization costs incurred within the first **three years** after purchase can become **anschaffungsnahe Herstellungskosten** if they exceed **15% of the building acquisition cost excluding VAT**.[4]

In plain English:

If you buy an older apartment and then spend heavily on fixing it up soon after purchase, you may **not** be allowed to deduct those costs immediately as current expenses.

Instead, they can get folded into the building cost and recovered only through depreciation.

This is a big deal because many buyers assume:

> I will renovate right after purchase and create a nice tax loss.

Sometimes the tax system says:

> No. You will depreciate that over time instead.

So if you buy an older flat in Berlin and plan early renovations, this rule must be part of your deal analysis.

---

## 9. Denkmalschutz: potentially helpful, but not automatic

It is common to hear that listed buildings are great because of tax advantages.

That is only partly true.

The enhanced monument depreciation rules under **§7i EStG** can apply to certain eligible measures on listed buildings, but this is tied to legal conditions and certification by the responsible monument authority.[9]

So the key point is:

**Buying a listed apartment does not automatically mean you get amazing tax deductions.**

Usually the extra tax benefit depends on:

- the type of measure
- whether it qualifies
- whether the authority certifies it
- how the costs are structured

So if a broker sells a listed building mainly with the phrase “great tax benefits,” that is a reason to slow down and verify everything carefully.

## 10. What “sensible price” actually means

This is where a lot of confusion disappears.

A sensible price is **not** the asking price.

It is also **not** “whatever similar apartments are listed for.”

A sensible price is:

> the price where the apartment still makes sense with realistic rent, realistic costs, realistic financing, and no fantasy assumptions.

### 10.1 A practical rule of thumb: price-to-rent multiple

One simple way to sanity-check a Berlin rental deal is to compare the price to the **annual net cold rent**.

Use this formula:

**Price-to-rent multiple = purchase price / annual cold rent**

My practical rule of thumb:

- **20–22x annual cold rent:** very interesting
- **23–25x:** maybe okay
- **26–28x:** getting expensive
- **29x+:** usually too expensive for a rental investment

This is not law. It is a practical investing filter.

### 10.2 Simple example

If current cold rent is **€800/month**:

- annual cold rent = **€9,600**
- 20x = **€192,000**
- 22x = **€211,200**
- 25x = **€240,000**
- 30x = **€288,000**

So if an already-rented apartment brings in €800 cold and the asking price is around **€290k–€310k**, it is usually expensive from an investor’s perspective.

If it is closer to **€200k–€230k**, it becomes more defendable.

### 10.3 For vacant apartments

A vacant apartment can justify a higher price than an already-rented one, because the achievable starting rent may be stronger.

But even then, the rent assumption should be:

- legally realistic
- operationally realistic
- not just broker fantasy

## 11. How to think about a deal in the right order

When you assess a Berlin rental apartment, think in this order:

### Step 1: Start with the real rent

Ask:

- What is the current cold rent?
- Is it already rented or vacant?
- If rented, how old is the tenancy?
- Is the actual rent far below current asking rents?

### Step 2: Add all the boring costs

Ask:

- What is the housegeld?
- How much of that is recoverable from the tenant?
- How much is reserve contribution?
- What are likely future repairs?
- Is there any sign of special assessments?

### Step 3: Add financing reality

Ask:

- What is the interest cost?
- What is the repayment burden?
- How much cash will leave my pocket every month?

### Step 4: Only then add the tax benefit

Ask:

- What part of the loss is actually deductible?
- What is the building share for AfA?
- Are there any special rules for listed status or renovation?
- Does the tax benefit improve the deal, or is it the only reason the deal looks acceptable?

That last question is the important one.

If the deal looks weak before tax and only “okay” because of tax savings, that is usually not a strong investment.

## 12. Red flags

These are the biggest warning signs based on what we discussed:

### Market / pricing red flags

- The apartment is already rented at a low rent, but the asking price assumes near-market new rent.
- The gross yield already looks weak before financing.
- The price-to-rent multiple is too high.
- The deal only works if Berlin prices keep rising strongly.

### Tax analysis red flags

- You are mainly buying to “get tax back.”
- You are counting principal repayment like it were a tax deduction.
- You assume all housegeld is deductible right away.
- You assume all early renovations will be immediately deductible.
- You assume Denkmalschutz automatically creates a tax jackpot.

### Building / operational red flags

- Old building with unclear repair backlog
- weak WEG documents
- high reserve needs
- possible special assessment risk
- long-standing tenant with low rent and limited upside

---

## 13. When a Berlin rental property can still make sense

Even after all the caution above, a Berlin rental can still be rational if several of these are true:

- you buy at a genuinely sensible price
- the rent is solid and real
- the building risk is understood
- your cash burn is manageable
- you have enough equity
- you plan to hold long term
- you treat tax benefits as support, not the main story

In that case, the investment can work as:

- a long-term asset
- a partial inflation hedge
- a diversification tool
- a tax-efficient but still fundamentally reasonable holding

---

## 14. My honest bottom line for your situation

Based on everything we discussed:

### What is true

- Your instinct that Berlin property feels expensive is valid.
- Your tax logic is also valid.
- Rental losses and depreciation can meaningfully reduce tax burden for a high-income earner.

### What is not safe to assume

- that tax savings alone justify the purchase
- that an overpriced apartment becomes smart because of deductions
- that a listed building automatically means exceptional tax treatment
- that housegeld and renovation costs will all help immediately in the way brokers imply

### My real conclusion

**A Berlin rental property can be worth considering for you, but only if the deal is still defensible before the tax story becomes the main argument.**

Or even more simply:

**A tax-efficient bad deal is still a bad deal.**

---

## 15. Practical checklist for the next apartment you evaluate

When you look at a listing, collect these numbers first:

1. Purchase price
2. Size in m²
3. Current cold rent
4. Warm rent and housegeld split
5. Whether the property is rented or vacant
6. Building year
7. Amount of reserve contribution in housegeld
8. Any special assessment history or repair backlog
9. Planned financing: interest rate, repayment, loan amount
10. Whether the building is under Denkmalschutz
11. Whether immediate renovation is needed

Then calculate:

- annual cold rent
- price-to-rent multiple
- gross yield
- monthly cash flow before tax
- estimated after-tax effect

And ask yourself this final question:

> If the tax benefit were smaller than I hope, would I still buy this apartment?

If the answer is no, that is usually your answer.
