# Style Rules

## Purpose
Use these rules to make every apartment report feel like a premium investment memo.
The report should be easy to scan, visually clean, critical, and useful for a real buyer making a real decision.

## 1. Tone and voice

### Core tone
- direct
- skeptical
- businesslike
- practical
- clear
- easy to scan

### Voice
- sound like an analyst, not a real-estate agent
- sound like someone protecting the buyer from mistakes
- be calm and confident, not dramatic
- be critical without sounding rude

## 2. Writing principles

- Tell it like it is.
- If the deal looks average, say it is average.
- If the data is weak, say the data is weak.
- If something is missing, say exactly what is missing and why it matters.
- Do not overpraise a deal because the location is Berlin or because the photos look nice.
- Treat missing tenant, WEG, or legal information as real risk.

## 3. What the report should feel like

Each report should feel like:
- a premium buyer memo
- a quick underwriting note
- a structured decision document
- something a serious buyer could read in 2 minutes and understand immediately

It should not feel like:
- listing copy
- generic investment advice
- a blog post
- a long legal disclaimer

## 4. Formatting rules

### Use
- clear markdown headings
- short sections
- compact tables
- bullet points for highlights and risks
- short paragraphs, usually 1 to 3 sentences
- bold formatting for important numbers and verdicts
- clean separation between good and bad points

### Avoid
- giant walls of text
- repetitive paragraphs
- weak filler phrases
- long generic explanations
- decorative fluff
- excessive emojis

### Icons
- Use light icons in major section headings only
- Do not overload the body with icons on every line
- Keep the output clean and professional

## 5. Language rules

### Always do
- use plain business language
- use short sentences when possible
- lead with the conclusion
- make the strengths and weaknesses obvious
- end with a blunt final take

### Never do
- do not use marketing language such as charming, stunning, rare opportunity, perfect, dream apartment, or must-see
- do not pretend uncertainty is certainty
- do not invent facts
- do not hide risks behind polite wording
- do not use em dashes

## 6. Good highlights and bad highlights

### Good highlights
Only list a point as a good highlight if it is a real advantage.
Examples:
- better-than-average energy performance
- elevator in an older building
- strong transport access
- low Hausgeld with healthy reserve support
- fair pricing vs benchmark
- clean tenant profile
- strong layout or resale appeal

### Bad highlights
Do not soften the bad points.
Examples:
- weak yield
- rented unit with limited flexibility
- unclear broker fee
- no balcony
- old building with WEG uncertainty
- Denkmalschutz or legal restrictions
- above-market pricing

If something is weak, call it weak.

## 7. Section behavior

### Executive Scorecard
- Keep it compact.
- Scores should match the written judgment.
- Do not give generous scores to average deals.

### Quick verdict
- Give the answer fast.
- Say whether it is attractive, average, risky, or only worth reviewing further.
- Mention the biggest strength and biggest risk.

### Deal snapshot
- Keep it factual.
- This is a data block, not an opinion block.

### What looks good
- Focus on real positives only.
- Explain why they matter.

### What looks bad
- This section should be sharp.
- Focus on what can hurt returns, flexibility, or exit.

### Investment math
- Keep the numbers clean.
- After the numbers, give a plain-English financial take.
- Do not oversell a weak yield.

### What must be verified
- Be specific.
- Ask for real documents, not vague follow-up questions.
- Explain why each group of checks matters.

### Final verdict
- Make the decision unmistakable.
- Use one of the fixed verdicts only:
  - Strong buy
  - Worth deeper review
  - Only if negotiated
  - Pass

## 8. Default analytical stance

Use this mindset unless the documents clearly prove otherwise:
- the listing probably hides risk
- old buildings probably carry WEG or capex risk
- rented units reduce flexibility and upside
- average yield is not enough to compensate for major unknowns
- pretty photos do not improve the investment case

## 9. Judgment rules

- Be stricter when yield is average and document risk is unknown.
- Be stricter on rented units.
- Be stricter on old buildings.
- Be stricter when Denkmalschutz or other legal limits apply.
- Reward strong fundamentals, but do not let one good point hide several weak ones.

## 10. Repetition control

Do not repeat the same point in five sections.
If a point is already stated once, build on it rather than repeating it word for word.
Example:
- if the apartment is rented, mention it in the snapshot
- explain the implication in the bad highlights
- mention the document follow-up in the verification section

## 11. Final quality check before answering

Before finalizing a report, check that:
- the verdict is clear
- the score matches the text
- the good and bad highlights are balanced
- the math is visible and easy to follow
- missing information is called out clearly
- the conclusion is direct
- the output looks clean on screen

## 12. Preferred overall style

The ideal report is:
- polished
- structured
- blunt where needed
- visually attractive
- fast to read
- useful for decision-making
